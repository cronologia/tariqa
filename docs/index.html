Tariqa Maryamiyya — Cronologia

Chronology of the Maryamiyya and the Traditionalist School (1886–present)

An open, source-referenced chronology of the Tariqa Maryamiyya — the Sufi order founded by Frithjof Schuon — and the wider Traditionalist School (Guénon–Schuon perennialism), with special attention to its documented connections: to Catholic traditionalism and to Brazil.

Last updated: 2026-07-20

About Chronology Key figures Organizations Disambiguation References

About

This is a work-in-progress dataset about a semi-private initiatic order and, in part, living people. It is compiled only from published scholarship (Mark Sedgwick's work is the academic backbone) and participants' own public writings; sympathetic, critical and apostate sources are labeled as such; contested characterizations are attributed, never asserted in the site's own voice; dates the sources dispute are flagged with a ? mark; and no private individuals are named. Corrections against published sources are welcome.

What it is
A Sufi order (tariqa) of Western origin founded by Frithjof Schuon, and the organizational heart of the Traditionalist School — the perennialist current of René Guénon, Schuon and Ananda Coomaraswamy.[13] [14]
Lineage
A branch of the Alawiyya of Shaykh Ahmad al-Alawi (Mostaganem, Algeria), where Schuon was initiated in the early 1930s; Schuon assumed the function of independent shaykh in Basel in late 1936 — an investiture his followers describe as divinely confirmed and Sedgwick treats as self-authorization.[13] [11] [1]
The name
'Maryamiyya' — after the Virgin Mary (Maryam), whom Schuon regarded as the order's spiritual protectress following visions he reported in 1965; the name was formally added in 1969 (full style: Tariqa Shadhiliyya-Darqawiyya-Alawiyya-Maryamiyya).[13]
Headquarters
Lausanne, Switzerland (1941–1980), then Bloomington, Indiana (1980–1998); since Schuon's retirement and death, autonomous zawiyas under individual khalifas — commonly described as Washington (Nasr) and London (Lings) branches, though Wikipedia's account stresses that no successor was named.[13] [17] [16]
In Brazil
A Brazilian branch founded in 1986/87 (sources differ) — described as the first tariqa brought to Brazil by Brazilians — with Olavo de Carvalho as Schuon's muqaddam until his break with Schuon at the end of the 1980s; centers reported at São José dos Campos (SP) with branch affiliations still to be verified. ?[4] [15]
Why it is in this project family
The documented crossovers: Rama Coomaraswamy — per Sedgwick never a formal Maryamiyya member, but a personal follower of Schuon who led a Catholic group practicing dhikr-like prayer with Christian concepts; also an SSPX seminary professor and sedevacantist priest — bridges to the fsspx project; Olavo de Carvalho (Maryami muqaddam turned philosopher of the Brazilian right) bridges to the fsp project's critical literature.[30] [10] [4]

Bloomington / São Paulo, Brazil The Brazilian branch founded — Olavo de Carvalho leads a group of Brazilians to Bloomington, is initiated (taking the name Sidi Muhammad Ibrahim, per the critical documentary blog, corroborated in outline by Sedgwick), and is appointed muqaddam in Brazil under Martin Lings's guidance — described as the first tariqa brought to Brazil by Brazilians. Sources split between 1986 (Sedgwick) and 1987 (Brazilian tertiary sources) for the founding.[4] [27] [15]
1989 1989 ? São Paulo, Brazil Olavo breaks with Schuon — Olavo de Carvalho leaves the order after a rupture with Schuon; the sources disagree on the date — 1987 (Bunker da Cultura), 'the late 1980s' (Sedgwick), or an expelled-1987–91 window (the Jardim series) — and on the reasons, with his own published framing (the multiconfessional rule, in 'Influências discretas') differing from the hostile accounts; every version is attributed. He reinvents himself, in Sedgwick's phrase, as a Catholic-identifying 'post-Traditionalist'.[4] [29] [27] [33]
1990s
1991 1991-10-15/11-20 Bloomington (Monroe County), Indiana, USA The Bloomington affair — indictment and dismissal — A grand jury indicts Schuon, 84, on allegations by a former member concerning ritual gatherings; the judge criticizes the indictment as vague, and on 20 November the county prosecutor dismisses all charges, stating there was insufficient evidence — 'not one shred of evidence' beyond the complainant's allegations, per the wording quoted by Wikipedia — adding that a miscarriage had occurred insofar as Schuon had been labeled. Schuon was never tried and the case was never revived. Accounts of the deputy prosecutor's subsequent departure differ (resigned vs dismissed) and both are attributed.[11] [26] [9]
1992 1992 Bloomington, Indiana, USA Schuon retires — Aged 85, Schuon resigns the direction of the order — per Wikipedia, without naming a successor, establishing instead independent zawiyas led by individual khalifas.[13]

Delphica; English translation 'Guénonian Esoterism and Christian Mystery', Sophia Perennis, 2004) — his public critique of Guénon's thesis on the Christian sacraments and of Schuon's supra-confessional esoterism. The Schuonian editorial by Renaud Fabbri (critical of Borella, labeled) reads him as having 'progressively distanced himself' from the school. Sources describe this departure as a gradual distancing rather than a dated break; this publication is its clearest datable public marker.[21] [40]
1998 1998-05-05 Bloomington, Indiana, USA Death of Frithjof Schuon — The order continues in autonomous branches, commonly identified with Seyyed Hossein Nasr (Washington) and Martin Lings (London) — a framing that coexists with the no-named-successor account.[11] [13]

Key figures

René Guénon (Abd al-Wahid Yahya)

1886–1951 · France / Egypt

Founder of the Traditionalist School; French metaphysician, Cairo-based Sufi from 1930; backed Vâlsan's 1950 separation from Schuon's branch.[12]

Frithjof Schuon (Isa Nur ad-Din Ahmad)

1907–1998 · Switzerland / USA

Founder and shaykh of the branch that became the Maryamiyya (1936–1992); leading perennialist author ('The Transcendent Unity of Religions').[11]

Ananda K. Coomaraswamy

1877–1947 · Ceylon / UK / USA

Art historian (Boston Museum of Fine Arts); with Guénon and Schuon one of the school's three founding authors. Exact biographical dates to verify.[14]

Rama P. Coomaraswamy

1929–2006 · USA

Son of Ananda; cardiac surgeon, later psychiatrist; professor at the SSPX's Ridgefield seminary until c. 1983, where he influenced the future SSPV founders; sedevacantist priest ordained in 1999 by Bishop José Ramón López-Gastón of the Thục line (per his own published account); contributor to the Foundation for Traditional Studies' journal Sophia. Per Sedgwick (Against the Modern World, p. 161) he was never a formal Maryamiyya member but a personal follower of Schuon who led a Catholic group practicing dhikr-like repetitive prayer with Christian concepts — see the fsspx project's dossier.[30] [31] [10] [24]

Titus Burckhardt (Ibrahim Izz ad-Din)

1908–1984 · Switzerland

Swiss author on sacred art and civilization; Schuon's closest lifelong collaborator.[20]

Martin Lings (Abu Bakr Siraj ad-Din)

1909–2005 · England

British Arabist and Shakespeare scholar, author of the celebrated 'Muhammad' (1983); head of the order's London line; advised the founding of the Brazilian branch.[16]

Seyyed Hossein Nasr

b. 1933 · Iran / USA

University Professor of Islamic Studies at George Washington University (from 1984); founder of the Foundation for Traditional Studies; head of the order's Washington line; the school's most prominent academic voice.[17]

Michel Vâlsan (Mustafa Abd al-Aziz)

1907–1974 · Romania / France

Romanian diplomat and esoterist; led the Paris Alawiyya and separated it from Schuon's branch in 1950 with Guénon's approval, founding the sharia-observant 'Guénonian-orthodox' line; editor of Études Traditionnelles. Exact biographical dates to verify.[18] [1]

Victor Danner

d. 1990 · USA

Indiana University professor who founded the Bloomington chapter (1967), seeding the community that became the order's headquarters. Biographical dates to verify.[19]

Whitall Perry

1920–2005 · USA

American compiler of 'A Treasury of Traditional Wisdom'; member of Schuon's circle in Lausanne and Bloomington.[22]

Huston Smith

1919–2016 · USA

Scholar of world religions ('The World's Religions'); joined the order in 1974 — with Nasr, its most famous academic follower per Sedgwick.[7]

Marco Pallis

1895–1989 · England

British-Greek mountaineer and author on Tibetan Buddhism; Traditionalist author. Biographical dates to verify.[14]

Jean Borella

b. 21 May 1930 · France

French Catholic philosopher; professor of metaphysics and ancient/medieval philosophy at the University of Nancy (Nancy-II) from 1977 until his 1995 retirement. Long counted a Traditionalist and a personal follower of Schuon: per Sedgwick (Against the Modern World, p. 161) he 'led a subsidiary group of perhaps fifty Catholic Traditionalists', of whom Sedgwick writes 'None of these Christians were actually members of the Maryamiyya, but they followed Schuon personally as a Maryami would' — a follower, on this account, not an initiate. Long-time co-editor of the journal Connaissance des religions (per Sedgwick's blog, June 2022); Philitt (2017) calls him a 'disciple' of Schuon. He later distanced himself from the school — criticizing Guénon on the Christian sacraments and Schuon's 'supra-confessional esoterism'. No source found asserts a formal initiation.[21] [39] [30] [42] [41] [43]

James S. Cutsinger

1953–2020 · USA

American scholar; Eastern Orthodox Christian; professor at the University of South Carolina (1980–2018) and editor/translator of several of Schuon's books. Per Sedgwick's blog (16 January 2023) he was 'a leading follower of Frithjof Schuon in a personal capacity, though never (as an Orthodox Christian) a formal member of the tariqa' — the same post associates him with Terry Moore and Hasan Awan's 2017 separation of a Washington–Baltimore branch. His documented office was secretary to the Foundation for Traditional Studies (a claim that he was 'Schuon's personal secretary' is unverified and not asserted here).[35] [46] [47]

Wolfgang Smith

1930–2024 · Austria / USA

Austrian-American mathematician, physicist and Catholic perennialist author; published by Angelico Press, with his collected works carried by his Philos-Sophia Initiative. Documented intellectual engagement with Nasr — Nasr reviewed his 'The Quantum Enigma' in the journal Sophia (1997) — but no source documents membership of any Sufi order: his relation to the school is one of affinity, not initiation. Sacred Web (a perennialist journal — sympathetic, labeled) records that he wished to be remembered 'as a believing Christian' and attended the Tridentine Mass.[48] [49] [50]

Charles Upton

b. 1948 (to verify) · USA

American poet and metaphysical author; adjacent to the Maryamiyya but, by his own 2017 'Confession' (self-disclosure), never a member of it. He and his wife joined the Nimatullahi Sufi Order under Javad Nurbakhsh in 1988; his wife Jennifer was later initiated into the Maryamiyya through Schuon's muqaddam Seyyed Hossein Nasr, 'though I remained with the Nimatullahis'; from 2010 he became an initiate of a separate order in an 'Alawiyya-descended silsila. He writes critically on the Schuon-scandal era. Never to be described as an 'ex-Maryami' — adjacency is not initiation.[36]

Olavo de Carvalho

1947–2022 · Brazil / USA

Brazilian essayist; joined the Maryamiyya in 1986, served as Schuon's muqaddam/khalifa in Brazil, broke with Schuon at the end of the 1980s, and became the Catholic-identifying philosopher of the Brazilian New Right — 'Brazil's most prominent post-Traditionalist' in Sedgwick's phrase; already documented as first-class critical literature in the sibling fsp project.[15] [4] [5]

Mateus Soares de Azevedo

1959– · Brazil

Brazilian perennialist author, journalist and translator into Portuguese of Schuon, Guénon, Lings, Stoddart and Rama Coomaraswamy; a World Wisdom author ('Men of a Single Book', 2010) and editor associated with the Schuonian/World Wisdom milieu. Felipe Souza's 2023 guest post calls him a 'prolific author' of 'Schuonian Traditionalism'. No membership of the Maryamiyya is self-disclosed or documented.[55] [34]

Alberto Vasconcellos Queiroz

b. 1963 (to verify) · Brazil

Brazilian psychologist and public administrator in São José dos Campos; co-editor (with Mateus Soares de Azevedo) of William Stoddart's 'Remembering in a World of Forgetting' (World Wisdom, 2007) and a perennialist author in Portuguese. Any initiatic affiliation [U] — not self-disclosed and never inferred here.[56]

Related organizations

Tariqa Alawiyya (parent order)

Founded c. 1914 · Mostaganem, Algeria

The Shadhili-Darqawi order of Shaykh Ahmad al-Alawi (d. 1934), parent of both Schuon's branch and Vâlsan's Paris line.[13]

Tariqa Maryamiyya

Founded 1936 / named 1969 · Basel → Lausanne → Bloomington

Schuon's branch: independent from 1936, Marian in devotion from 1965, formally 'Maryamiyya' from 1969; headquartered in Lausanne 1941–1980 and Bloomington 1980–1998; decentralized after Schuon's retirement into autonomous zawiyas.[13]

The Vâlsan / Paris line

Founded 1950 · Paris, France

The independent, sharia-observant Alawiyya branch Michel Vâlsan established with Guénon's approval after objecting to Schuon's universalist adaptations — the 'Guénonian-orthodox' alternative within the same lineage; not Maryami.[1] [18]

Bloomington community

Founded 1967 · Bloomington, Indiana, USA

Founded around Victor Danner's chapter; the order's world headquarters 1980–1998; the setting of the practices analyzed in Hugh Urban's study and of the 1991 affair (see the chronology's paired entry).[19] [9]

Washington branch (Nasr line)

Washington, DC, USA

The line around Seyyed Hossein Nasr, George Washington University, and the Foundation for Traditional Studies (journal Sophia).[17]

London branch (Lings line)

London, England

The line around Martin Lings until his death in 2005.[16]

Brazilian centers

Founded 1986/87 · São Paulo state, Brazil

The branch founded under Olavo de Carvalho's muqaddam years. Per the abstract of Sedgwick's Aries article, two local groups developed in 1980s–90s Brazil — one restricting itself to Traditionalist doctrine, the other turning to T'ai chi and Brazilian indigenous religion rather than Sufism. Brazilian sources describe two centers — one at São José dos Campos (SP) — affiliated respectively to the London (Lings) and Washington (Nasr) lines; the full article (paywalled) is the key document to obtain (issue #1).[3] [4] [33]

Founding year disputed between 1986 (Sedgwick) and 1987 (Brazilian sources).

World Wisdom (publisher)

Founded 1980 · Bloomington, Indiana, USA

The publishing house of the Schuon circle — Schuon, Nasr, Lings, Burckhardt, the 'Library of Perennial Philosophy'. Participant-run: label sympathetic when citing.[23]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Wisdom

Foundation for Traditional Studies / journal Sophia

Founded 1984 · Washington, DC, USA

Nasr's foundation and journal — the academic-facing organ of the school; Rama Coomaraswamy was a member and contributor.[17] [10]

Angelico Press / Sophia Perennis (publishers)

Founded 2011 · Brooklyn, New York, USA

The documented institutional bridge between Guénonian Traditionalism and traditional-Catholic publishing. Angelico Press was founded in the fall of 2011 by John Riess and James Wetmore (per Riess's own account, California Catholic Daily, 3 April 2013; both named as founders on the current about-us page). Wetmore is director of Sophia Perennis and translator/editor of volumes of the Collected Works of René Guénon. Mark Sedgwick (30 June 2022) describes Angelico as 'as much a (Guénonian) Traditionalist publisher that became popular among traditional Catholics as it was a traditional Catholic publisher that published (Guénonian) Traditionalists' — 'an overlap or even a mixing of two formerly distinct streams'. Sedgwick documents that in 2020 Angelico's site listed a joint Angelico Press / Sophia Perennis imprint (16 books) alongside a purely Sophia Perennis imprint (3 books), since removed from the site. The school-of-ideas side of Sophia Perennis is documented in the sibling perennialism project — linked here, not duplicated.[42] [44] [45]

Riess (2013) describes starting the press 'with two colleagues'; the current site names two founders — a possible third founder is unidentified (recorded, not resolved). sophiaperennis.com currently resolves to a parking page (observed 19 July 2026); live status unknown.

Disambiguation & nuance

This subject sits at the crossing of three different things called 'traditionalism', a semi-private religious order, and living people. The entries below keep the wires uncrossed; every characterization is attributed.

Traditionalist School ≠ Catholic traditionalism

The Guénon–Schuon school (perennialism) is unrelated in origin to Catholic traditionalism (Lefebvre, Écône, sedevacantism — the fsspx project's subject). The documented bridges are individuals: Rama Coomaraswamy, Jean Borella, and Olavo de Carvalho. Guénon himself began among French Catholic intellectuals and broke with them ('Orient et Occident', 1924).[14] [10] [21]

≠ Evola's political 'Traditionalism'

Julius Evola adapted Guénon toward radical-right politics; Schuon's line was religious-initiatic, not political. The later Bannon/Dugin/alt-right 'Traditionalism' story connects to the Evolian reading — Sedgwick notes Olavo de Carvalho 'applied Guénon to political issues rather as Evola had, but unlike Evola combined Traditionalism with Roman Catholicism'. Attribute all such lineage claims.[4] [14]

Maryamiyya ≠ mainstream Sufism

Criticism of Schuon's universalist adaptations began inside the Guénonian world itself — the 1950 Vâlsan split over the relaxation of Islamic practice — and continued over the Marian devotion, the Plains Indian rites and the Bloomington gatherings. Defenders present these as legitimate esoteric adaptation; critics as syncretism. Both framings are attributed.[1] [9] [13]

Academic perennialism vs initiatic membership

Perennialism was taught openly in universities (Nasr, Smith) while tariqa membership remained private. This site keeps the published-scholarship layer separate from claims about membership, and carries no information about private living members — deceased figures and self-disclosed members only.[17] [7]

The 1991 Bloomington affair carries its resolution

Any mention of the 15 October 1991 indictment must carry the 20 November 1991 dismissal of all charges, the prosecutor's insufficient-evidence statements, and the fact that Schuon was never tried and the case never revived. The chronology's single paired entry is the pattern to follow.[11] [26]

Names and nicknames

'Guru de Virgínia' is a Brazilian press nickname for Olavo de Carvalho (from his Virginia residence, 2005–2022), not a title about Schuon. The order's full style is Tariqa Shadhiliyya-Darqawiyya-Alawiyya-Maryamiyya; 'Maryamiyya' alone is standard in scholarship.[15] [13]

Follower ≠ member: the 'Christian Schuonians'

Sedgwick documents a category the record keeps flattening: Christians who followed Schuon personally without ever joining the tariqa — Rama Coomaraswamy's American Catholic group ('none of these Christians were actually members of the Maryamiyya'), probably Jean Borella's French circle, and the Orthodox scholar James Cutsinger, Schuon's editor, 'never (as an Orthodox Christian) a formal member'. Membership claims about individuals require self-disclosure or scholarship — adjacency is not initiation.[30] [35] [36]

Brazil's Guénonian scene today (attributed to Felipe Souza, 2023)

A 2023 guest post by Felipe Souza on Mark Sedgwick's Traditionalists blog — authored by Souza, not Sedgwick — maps a Portuguese-language Guénonian milieu: the René Guénon Institute for Traditional Studies (IRGET), run by the late Luiz Pontual de Oliveira; translator-teachers Marcelo Cipolla and the História Islâmica portal (Mansur Peixoto); and Luiz Gonzaga de Carvalho, Olavo's son, said to attend the classes. These roles rest on Souza's single account and are recorded as attributed, not confirmed. Souza also situates Nova Resistência (Raphael Machado) as a Duginist movement that 'flirts with Guénonism' — that is Evola/Dugin-line political Traditionalism (the 'Fourth Political Theory'), kept apart from the Schuon school per this project's three-traditionalisms rule; it is not the Maryamiyya's initiatic line.[34]
